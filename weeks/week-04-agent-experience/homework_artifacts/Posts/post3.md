One thought keeps coming back while exploring 𝗣𝗹𝗮𝗶𝗻𝗧𝗿𝗮𝗰𝗸.

Most people don't start a new job thinking: "I'm going to keep terrible records and spend more time than necessary fighting with time bookkeeping software."

Yet somehow, when work gets busy, documentation is often one of the first things that suffers.
❌ Not because the work became less important.
❌ Not because people suddenly stopped caring.
⚙️ But because documenting the work often becomes disconnected from doing the work.

You do the work ➡️ document the work ➡️ categorize the work ➡️ estimate the work ➡️ report the work.
And when the day is already full, the detailed trail starts to shrink.

What often remains is the minimum viable record:
➖ "Worked on project X."
➖ "About two hours."
➖ "Most of the afternoon."
➖ "Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet ..."

Enough to appease the time bookkeeping process ❓
Not always enough to understand what actually happened later ❗

I have started calling this a 𝘁𝗶𝗺𝗲'𝘀𝘁𝗶𝗺𝗮𝘁𝗲.
▪️ Part time entry
▪️ Part estimate
▪️ Part reconstruction

It is not necessarily wrong.
Sometimes it is the only realistic thing left at the end of a busy day.

But it does show something interesting: When pressure increases, measurement often survives.
▪️ Context does not.
▪️ The hours are still there.
▪️ The allocation may still be there.
▪️ The reportable number may still be there.
▪️ But the small fragments that explain the work begin to disappear.

If documentation gives meaning to measurement, how can documentation be designed so it survives busy workdays?
As something small enough to stay close to the work itself.

I'm not sure where that thought leads yet, but it feels like a more interesting question than simply asking how to track time more accurately.
