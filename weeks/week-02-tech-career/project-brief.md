## Snapshot

**Working title:** Open Source Readiness Field Guide

**First tangible artifact:** Rough research map / source inventory

## Project continuation

This is the same project direction as Week 01: an **Open Source Readiness Field Guide** for organizations that already depend on open source but may not yet have the language, structure, or confidence to engage with it intentionally.

The Week 02 change is not a project pivot. It is a narrowing of the next useful step.

Instead of jumping directly into a polished guide, the project moved into a research and curation phase: collecting existing open source enablement material, comparing what already exists and trying to understand which parts should be reused, referenced, summarized, or left outside the first version.

## Current project direction

The project is currently less in builder mode and more in discovery mode.

The working metaphor for this week is: **truffle pig rather than builder**.

The goal is to find existing puzzle pieces before creating new ones. There is already a lot of open source material available. The immediate question is not “What can I write from scratch?” but:

> What already exists, what is useful for the target audience and how could it be connected into a practical field guide?

## Audience status

The long-term audience is still small and midsize organizations, especially companies that are becoming more software-driven but do not have mature open source structures.

However, this week I deliberately stepped back from formal stakeholder and customer research. The immediate task is to understand the existing open source enablement landscape first.

That means Week 02 is not primarily about user interviews, validation, or persona work. It is about building enough source awareness to avoid reinventing the wheel.

## Problem I am trying to solve this week

The project tension became clearer this week.

I want to avoid a guide that only covers low-hanging fruit such as “What is Git?”, “What is a repository?”, or “What is source control?” At the same time, I do want to explain the deeper value behind those basics: collaboration around one source of truth, transparent history and different strategies for combining work products.

I also want to avoid pretending that I can provide legal advice around software licenses, intellectual property, or ownership. At the same time, licensing and intellectual property are part of what makes open source powerful, differentiated and risky when misunderstood.

So the project needs to find a careful middle ground:

- practical without becoming shallow
- useful without pretending to be authoritative in legal or compliance areas
- beginner-friendly without reducing open source to basic tooling vocabulary
- grounded in existing material without becoming only a link list

## Blockers and risks

### Scope bloat

The more I break down the project, the more topics appear. Every chunk seems to become its own set of sub-chunks.

This may be inherent early scope bloat, but it is still a risk. The field guide could easily expand into history, tooling, licensing, governance, sustainability, contribution models, software supply chain, SBOMs, organizational readiness, community participation and innovation strategy all at once.

### Authority boundary

I am not an OSPO. I am deeply interested in the topic, but I need to avoid writing with false authority in areas that require domain expertise.

That makes expert feedback important, especially from organizational OSPOs and people already working in open source governance, compliance and enablement.

### Research clutter

AI made it easy to digest many resources quickly, but not easy to make strategic decisions. The more links and summaries I collected, the more clutter appeared. The hard part is not finding material. The hard part is deciding what the yellow line through the material should be.

## Decisions made this week

- Keep the project umbrella as **Open Source Readiness Field Guide**.
- Narrow the Week 02 focus to **open source enablement research and material curation**.
- Treat the first tangible artifact as a rough **research map / source inventory**.
- Avoid formal stakeholder and customer research for now.
- Reuse and connect existing material where possible instead of reinventing it.
- Spar with organizational OSPO contacts before turning the idea into a public GitHub skeleton.

## Decisions postponed

- Creating a dedicated GitHub skeleton for the field guide idea.
- Selecting the final structure of the guide.
- Deciding which topics belong in the first version.
- Choosing the first public artifact beyond the weekly learning log.
- Defining how deeply to include licensing, intellectual property, compliance, and software supply chain topics.

## Week 02 retrospective

### What worked

A brainstorming funnel helped channel the idea flow and capture individual blocks of what I envision.

AI helped create fast first-pass overviews of the source material and turn different resources into more comparable summaries.

### What felt unclear

The big-picture structure is still missing. I can identify many possible puzzle pieces, but not yet the final shape they should form.

### What I would change next week

Reflect on the idea with people inside the business and challenge the scope with someone who has more open source governance or OSPO experience.

### What I learned about the project

A rough idea can create a lot of work once it is broken into real pieces.

The project may be less about writing original content than about curation, framing, sequencing and knowing where not to overstep.

## Next useful step

Create a first structured source inventory that compares resources across a few dimensions, for example:

- audience
- topic coverage
- depth
- maturity
- public accessibility
- reuse potential
- risk or authority boundary
- relevance to small and midsize organizations

That source inventory should become the basis for deciding the first chapter or first public artifact of the field guide.
