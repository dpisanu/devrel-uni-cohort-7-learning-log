# Public Reflection: Week 02 — Inspiration, Pressure, and Scope Control

Week 02 was energizing and difficult at the same time.

The session with Nader Dabit was inspiring because it framed career growth as something broader than a job title. The idea of staying adaptable as a technologist, building visible work, growing a network before it is urgently needed, and creating career optionality resonated strongly with me.

But it also created pressure.

The keynote opened many new paths at once: learning systems, AI workflows, public work, DevRel career thinking, networking, experimentation, and personal reinvention. All of that is useful. All of it is interesting. And all of it can become a rabbit hole.

## What changed in my thinking

I realized that I have too much on my plate.

Compared to some cohort peers, I feel far away from a tangible product. The project idea is still broad and every attempt to break it down creates more subtopics.

This week made it clear that the next step cannot be “build the whole field guide.” It needs to be smaller and more structured.

The current project step is now:

> Build a research map and source inventory for open source enablement material.

That feels less glamorous than a polished artifact, but it is more honest.

## What surprised me

I was surprised by how cluttered everything became once I started breaking down the idea and how many areas are currently above my comfort level.

At first, “Open Source Readiness Field Guide” sounds like one project. But under the surface are many possible areas:

- open source basics
- collaboration models
- licensing awareness
- intellectual property boundaries
- compliance risks
- software supply chain
- OSPO structures
- contribution models
- sustainability
- community participation
- organizational enablement

Each of these could become its own rabbit hole.

## What challenged me

Time management became the real challenge.

I expected to work on the project in manageable slots. In reality, research and sense-making took much longer than expected.

AI helped with fast summaries and initial comparisons. It made the first pass easier. But it did not magically create clarity. In some cases, adding more sources just created more clutter.

The harder work is deciding what matters.

## How this connected back to Week 01

Week 01 helped me frame the project as an Open Source Readiness Field Guide.

Week 02 challenged that framing by asking me to think not only about the project, but also about career direction and DevRel identity.

That created a tension:

- Do I focus on learning DevRel etiquette and what it means to be in DevRel?
- Or do I put my head down and work on the project, applying DevRel practices where I can?

The answer is probably not either/or. But for the next week, I need to avoid letting career reflection consume the project work.

## What I want to carry into Week 03

Slow it down.

I do not need to chase every interesting signal. I am not missing a special train for the next job. The point of this cohort is to get a feel for the DevRel landscape, reflect on what it means for me, and keep moving the project forward in small, useful steps.

For the project, that means:

- keep the umbrella: Open Source Readiness Field Guide
- narrow the current work: open source enablement research and material curation
- make the first artifact: research map / source inventory
- ask for expert feedback before overbuilding
- avoid pretending to be authoritative where I am not

## One sentence takeaway

Week 02 reminded me that inspiration is useful, but focus is what turns it into progress.
