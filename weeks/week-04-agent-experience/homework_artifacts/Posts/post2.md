After introducing my [DevRel Uni](https://www.linkedin.com/company/devrel-uni/) Capstone project 𝗣𝗹𝗮𝗶𝗻𝗧𝗿𝗮𝗰𝗸 yesterday, I've been reflecting on why 🖋️ documenting work feels different from 📏 measuring work.

Most work environments eventually require some form of measurement.
🕝 Hours worked
⏱️ Flex time
🏝️ Vacation
⌛ Project allocation
📅 Monthly reports

Those 🔢 numbers 𝗺𝗮𝘁𝘁𝗲𝗿.
But numbers alone 𝚛̲𝚊̲𝚛̲𝚎̲𝚕̲𝚢̲ tell a story about the work itself.
A report will tell me that I spent 8 hours on a particular day.

It won't tell me:
➖ Which deployment kept me busy
➖ Which customer conversation changed my priorities
➖ Which document helped unblock the next step

Those are the fragments that usually 𝚍̲𝚒̲𝚜̲𝚊̲𝚙̲𝚙̲𝚎̲𝚊̲𝚛̲ ̲𝚏̲𝚒̲𝚛̲𝚜̲𝚝̲.
Yet they these parts are useful later when trying to understand what actually happened. Not down to the last detail but from an 🦅 eagle perspective.

The more I think about it, the more I see 𝘁𝘄𝗼 𝗱𝗶𝗳𝗳𝗲𝗿𝗲𝗻𝘁 𝗮𝗿𝘁𝗶𝗳𝗮𝗰𝘁𝘀 emerging from the same work:

📍 Documentation
⋅ the trail
⋅ the breadcrumbs
⋅ the story of what happened

📊 Measurement
⋅ the totals
⋅ the allocation
⋅ the reportable numbers

Neither is sufficient on its own.
📃 Documentation without measurement becomes a diary.
📐 Measurement without documentation becomes a spreadsheet.

A new interesting topic coming up when reflecting about 𝗣𝗹𝗮𝗶𝗻𝗧𝗿𝗮𝗰𝗸 is the relationship between the two.

❌ Not how to measure more
❌ Not how to document more
✅ But how documentation can provide meaning to measurement

How 𝚜̲𝚖̲𝚊̲𝚕̲𝚕̲ ̲𝚏̲𝚛̲𝚊̲𝚐̲𝚖̲𝚎̲𝚗̲𝚝̲𝚜̲ recorded along the way can later become both a story of the work and a useful report of the time spent.

I'm still exploring this idea, but it feels like one of the more interesting questions hiding underneath what initially looked like a simple time documentation problem.
