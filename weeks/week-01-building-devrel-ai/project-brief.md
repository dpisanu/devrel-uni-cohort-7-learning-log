# Project Brief: Week 01 — Open Source Readiness Field Guide

## Snapshot

**Week:** 01  
**Working title:** Open Source Readiness Field Guide  
**Project status:** Framed, not yet validated  
**Current stage:** Narrowing a broad interest into a first useful artifact

## Project idea

The project I framed during Week 01 is an **Open Source Readiness Field Guide**.

The idea is to create a practical learning and orientation resource for small and midsize companies that already depend on open source but may not yet have the language, structures, or confidence to engage with it intentionally.

The guide should help companies understand open source as more than “free software.” It should make topics such as usage, responsibility, license awareness, compliance, software supply chain, sustainability, contribution, and good citizenship easier to approach.

## Audience

The primary audience is small and midsize companies in my region, especially family-owned or traditionally industrial companies that are becoming more software-driven.

Potential readers include:

- Developers
- Engineering managers
- Technical leads
- Product teams
- Innovation teams
- Compliance-adjacent stakeholders
- Business leaders who want to understand open source more strategically

A secondary audience is the local open source and OSPO community, because the project could become a bridge between experienced practitioners and companies that are still at the beginning of their open source journey.

## Problem statement

Many companies already depend on open source, but not all of them understand what that dependency means.

Some companies may encounter open source mainly through compliance obligations, audits, customer requirements, or software supply chain concerns. Others may benefit from open source every day without having a clear strategy for responsible usage, contribution, or sustainability.

The problem I want to explore is:

> How can open source readiness become approachable for small and midsize companies without oversimplifying the real responsibilities around licenses, compliance, software supply chain, sustainability, and community participation?

## Project boundary

This project is not intended to be:

- Legal advice
- A compliance framework
- A replacement for an Open Source Program Office
- A complete governance model
- A definitive guide to all open source topics

It is intended to be a beginner-friendly orientation resource that helps companies ask better questions and take a more intentional first step.

## Why this matters to me

This project connects several topics I care about: Developer Relations, open source, education, community building, and regional industry enablement.

Before Week 01, I had recently attended a Linux Foundation event focused on open source in industry and completed the Open Source Software Developer certification. Those experiences made me want to engage more deeply with the OSPO community and with the question of how open source knowledge can reach companies that may not yet have formal open source structures.

Week 01 helped me turn that broad interest into a project shape that I can explain and iterate on.

## Week 01 decisions

During Week 01, I made several framing decisions:

- Start with a field guide instead of a full framework.
- Focus on small and midsize companies rather than large organizations with mature OSPO structures.
- Treat the project as an enablement artifact, not as a legal or compliance authority.
- Use the first version as a conversation starter.
- Keep the tone practical, beginner-friendly, and grounded.
- Ask for feedback from people with more OSPO and open source experience.

## Week 01 retrospective

### What became clearer

The project does not need to solve the entire open source readiness problem at once. The first useful version can be smaller: a practical entry point that helps a specific audience understand the first concepts, risks, opportunities, and questions.

### What changed

Before Week 01, the idea was mostly a broad interest in open source, OSPO work, and regional knowledge sharing. After the project-framing exercise, it became a more concrete artifact: an Open Source Readiness Field Guide.

### What still feels uncertain

- Should the guide primarily serve companies, individuals inside companies, or the local open source community?
- What is the smallest useful version that can be finished during the cohort?
- How can I keep the guide approachable without making complex topics sound too simple?
- Which topics belong in the first version, and which should be left for later?
- How much should the project connect to the five-week cohort, and how much should it stand on its own?

### Risks to watch

- Making the scope too broad again.
- Sounding authoritative in areas that require legal, compliance, or domain-specific expertise.
- Writing for “everyone” instead of a clear first audience.
- Producing a document instead of creating a conversation starter.

### Next useful step

Draft the first outline of the field guide and identify the first set of questions a small or midsize company might ask when it realizes that open source is not only a technical dependency, but also a responsibility and ecosystem relationship.
