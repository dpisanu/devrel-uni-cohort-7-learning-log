## Post draft

### LinkedIn

Week 02 of DevRel University was both inspiring and overwhelming.

Nader Dabit’s session on navigating a tech career in an industry that will not slow down strongly resonated with me. What resonated most with me was the idea of not putting yourself into a fixed professional drawer and then staying there in stasis.

That has been a recurring theme in my own career as well and I especially liked the framing of being more of a technologist: someone who keeps learning, builds visible work, grows a network and stays open to new opportunities.

At the same time, it added pressure.

The session opened a lot of new doors: DevRel career thinking, learning systems, public work, AI workflows, networking and personal reinvention. All useful. All interesting. All tempting rabbit holes.

That created a real tension for me this week: 
1️⃣ Do I focus on learning DevRel etiquette and what it means to work in DevRel?
2️⃣ Or do I put my head down, continue the project work, and apply DevRel practices as I go?

For my Open Source Readiness Field Guide project, the practical answer this week was to narrow the scope.

Instead of trying to build a polished guide immediately, I started with open source enablement research and material curation: collecting resources, comparing what already exists and building a rough research map / source inventory.

AI helped a lot with fast summaries and first-pass comparisons. I also used a local Whisper-based transcript workflow while reviewing the session, which made it much easier to find specific sections in my notes.

But AI did not solve the hard part: judgment and decision taking.

So, week 02 was an earthquake on time management and resource allocation.

The lesson I am taking forward: inspiration is useful, but focus is what turns it into progress.
