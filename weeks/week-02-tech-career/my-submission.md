# My Submission: Week 02 — Choosing Direction & Taking Action

## Context

Week 02 asked us to define our direction more concretely, build a first tangible version, reflect on the process and share progress publicly.

My project is still the **Open Source Readiness Field Guide**. The goal is to create an approachable orientation resource for organizations that already depend on open source but may not yet have the language, structure, or confidence to engage with it intentionally.

The Week 02 shift is that I am not trying to build the polished guide yet. The immediate focus is **open source enablement research and material curation**.

## Part 1 — Career Direction Applied to My Project

### What opportunities do I see emerging?

I see an opportunity around open source enablement for organizations that are becoming more software-driven but do not yet have mature open source structures.

Many companies already depend on open source. Some understand this mainly through compliance, audits, customer expectations, or software supply chain requirements. Others benefit from open source every day without having a clear language for stewardship, contribution, sustainability, or community participation.

At the same time, there is already a large amount of open source enablement material available from organizations, foundations, training providers and community projects.

The opportunity I see is not necessarily to create everything from scratch. It is to help connect existing material into a practical path that makes open source readiness more approachable for small and midsize organizations.

### Which opportunity am I choosing to focus on?

For Week 02, I am choosing to focus on **research mapping and source curation** for the Open Source Readiness Field Guide.

This means:

- finding existing open source enablement resources
- comparing what they cover
- identifying which resources are mature and reusable
- understanding where the gaps are
- avoiding duplication where good material already exists
- preparing the ground for a smaller first version of the field guide

The first tangible direction is a rough **research map / source inventory**, not a polished guide or full GitHub project skeleton.

### What skills will I intentionally develop through this project?

I want to develop:

- better explanatory writing
- better source curation
- building in public without over-polishing
- AI-assisted research workflows
- the ability to turn scattered notes into a structured learning path
- better judgment around where I can explain a topic myself and where I should point to existing expertise
- a stronger understanding of security and prompting risks when using AI-assisted workflows

### What am I choosing not to focus on?

For now, I am deliberately not focusing on formal stakeholder or customer research.

That does not mean the audience is unimportant. The long-term audience still matters. But right now I need to understand the existing open source enablement landscape before I start validating a specific format with others.

I am also choosing not to:

- build a polished guide yet
- create a public GitHub skeleton before the structure is clearer
- reinvent existing OSPO or open source training material
- chase every AI tooling trend that helps me summarize and create fancy presentations
- turn the project into legal advice or a compliance framework

## Part 2 — Build Something: First Iteration

### What did I build this week?

I built the first rough version of a research map and source inventory.

In practice, this currently consists of:

- a long link collection

- AI-assisted first-pass summaries
- early comparison of different providers and resources
- notes about which resources may be mature enough to become references

It is not polished yet but it is a tangible first iteration. It gives me something to sort, challenge and improve upon.

### What was easier than expected?

The initial digest of most providers was easier than expected.

Using AI, I could get a quick rough overview of many resources in a short amount of time. Summarization and initial comparison were relatively simple.

The initial link and provider research took around two hours.

### What was harder than expected?

Making sense of the material was harder than expected.

The AI did not magically turn the link collection into a clear structure. The more links I added and the more deep dives I attempted, the more cluttered the results became.

The difficult part was giving enough direction through the prompt to do something useful with the data. I still need to define the “yellow line” through the material.

Sifting through the results took most of a day and is still not finished.

### Where did AI help most?

AI helped most with:

- fast content consumption
- first-pass summarization
- initial comparison
- turning different source materials into more uniform and comparable datasets
- helping me navigate transcripts and notes more quickly

However, AI helped less with the deeper judgment work.

So far it was great to summarize resources, but it does not automatically know what I am actually looking for. I still need to decide which resources matter, what belongs in the first version and where the project boundaries are. Also create a clear understanding of what is really important and what not in regards to what I want to achieve. Maybe time for a round of priorization of goals.

## Part 3 — Reflection

### How long did it take to go from idea to first version?

The first link and provider research took about two hours.

Turning that into something meaningful took much longer. Sifting through the results, comparing resources, and trying to understand what belongs in the field guide took most of a day and is still unfinished.

So the first tangible version came quickly, but the first useful structure did not.

### Did AI change how fast or how well I built?

AI changed the speed of the early research phase. It helped me move through sources faster than I could have done manually.

But it did not remove the hard part.

The hard part is not collecting links or summarizing pages. The hard part is judgment:

- What do I really want to accomplish?
- What is relevant for the intended audience?
- What should be reused?
- What should be summarized?
- What should be excluded?
- Which topics require expert review?
- Where do I need to avoid sounding more authoritative than I am?

AI accelerated consumption, but it did not replace sense-making.

### The tension I am noticing

I am in a tension place around the scope of the field guide.

On one side, I want to avoid low-hanging fruit such as basic explanations of repositories, Git, or source control. On the other side, it is important to explain why those practices matter: collaboration through one source of truth, transparent work history and different strategies for combining work products.

Similarly, I want to avoid diving too deeply into the legal area of software licenses, intellectual property, and ownership. But these topics are central to open source. They are a key area of strength and differentiation and also an area where organizations can create real pitfalls if they misunderstand the basics.

This means the project has to be careful.

It should not become a basic Git tutorial. It should not become legal advice. It should not pretend to be an OSPO playbook.

But it still needs to show why collaboration models, licensing awareness, stewardship and sustainability matter.

### What this tells me about the project

The project is not only about writing content.

It is about:

- curation
- framing
- sequencing
- knowing what to leave out
- knowing when to point to existing experts
- creating an approachable entry point without flattening the complexity

That is harder than I expected.
