# My Submission: Week 01 — Framing My Project

## Context

Going into Week 01 of DevRel University Cohort 7, I already had an idea of a topic in mind.

Just before the program started, I attended a Linux Foundation event focused on open source in industry. The event was organized by a core group of Open Source Program Officers and Open Source Officers from my region. It focused on compliance, processes, licenses, Software Bills of Materials, open source governance, and the role of open source in the software supply chain.

Shortly after that event, I completed my Open Source Software Developer certification through the Linux Foundation. Both experiences gave me a strong urge to become more engaged with the Open Source Program Office community and with the broader question of how companies participate in open source responsibly.

At the same time, there is a state-supported regional initiative that aims to bring small and midsize companies together around open source knowledge. Many of these companies are family-owned, regionally rooted, and part of the industrial backbone of the area. That combination made the topic feel concrete to me.

Open source is already part of how these companies build software, products, and internal systems. But knowledge around open source usage, compliance, contribution, sustainability, and good citizenship is not always evenly distributed. That is the area I brought into Week 01 as my project direction.

## What am I building?

I want to build the first parts of an **Open Source Readiness Field Guide**.

The idea is to create a practical learning and orientation resource for small and midsize companies that want to understand what responsible open source participation can look like.

This is not meant to be a complete legal, compliance, or governance framework. It is also not meant to replace the work of an Open Source Program Office. Instead, I want it to be an approachable starting point for companies that are already using open source but may not yet have the language, structure, or confidence to engage with it intentionally.

The guide should help explain topics such as:

- What open source enables for companies
- Why open source is more than “free software”
- What it means to be a good open source citizen
- Why licenses and compliance matter
- Why Software Bills of Materials matter
- How open source connects to software supply chain topics
- Why sustainability matters when companies depend on open source projects
- How smaller companies can start building awareness without being overwhelmed

## Who is it for?

The primary audience is small and midsize companies in my region, especially family-owned or traditionally industrial companies that are becoming more software-driven.

Within those companies, the guide could be useful for:

- Developers
- Engineering managers
- Technical leads
- Product teams
- Innovation teams
- Compliance-adjacent stakeholders
- Business leaders who want to understand open source more strategically

A secondary audience is the local open source and OSPO community, because this project could help create a bridge between people who already work deeply in open source and companies that are still at the beginning of that journey.

## What problem does it solve?

Many companies already depend on open source, but not all of them have a clear understanding of what that dependency means.

Some may see open source mainly as free software they can use. Others may only encounter open source through compliance obligations, customer requirements, audits, or software supply chain concerns. This can make open source feel either invisible or intimidating.

The problem I want to explore is how to make open source participation more understandable and actionable for smaller companies.

I want to help answer:

- Where should a company start?
- What should a company know before open source becomes a risk topic?
- How can a company move from passive usage to responsible participation?
- What does “good open source citizenship” mean in practice?
- How can open source be framed not only as a compliance issue, but also as a capability, culture, and community topic?

## Why does this project matter to me personally?

This project matters to me because it connects several things I care about: Developer Relations, open source, education, community building, and the role of technology in regional industry.

The Linux Foundation event made me realize how much important open source knowledge already exists in the OSPO community. At the same time, I saw how valuable it would be to make some of that knowledge more accessible to companies that do not yet have dedicated open source structures.

Completing the Open Source Software Developer certification strengthened that motivation. It made me want to move from learning about open source toward contributing to open source education and enablement.

The regional context also matters to me. Small and midsize companies are an important part of the local economy. If they depend on open source, then helping them understand responsible usage, contribution, and sustainability feels meaningful.

For me, this is not only a technical topic. It is also about trust, responsibility, collaboration, and long-term ecosystem health.

## What actions can I take to make sure I succeed?

For Week 01, the most important action was to narrow the idea down from a broad interest in open source and OSPO work into something more concrete.

The Miro exercise helped with that. The original idea was broad: I wanted to become more engaged with the OSPO community and support open source knowledge sharing in my region. The smaller version I can work on is a first draft of an Open Source Readiness Field Guide.

To make progress, I can:

- Write down the target audience clearly.
- Identify the first questions SMEs usually have about open source.
- Collect the most important terms that need explanation.
- Draft a simple structure for the guide.
- Start with my own understanding rather than trying to make it complete.
- Ask open source practitioners for feedback.
- Share early drafts publicly.
- Keep the tone practical, beginner-friendly, and grounded.

## What would success look like?

At this stage, success is not about having a complete guide.

Success means that I can translate my broad interest into a concrete project direction that I can explain to myself, my wife, and others.

By the end of this first framing step, it should include:

- A clear project title
- A clear audience
- A clear problem statement
- A first explanation of why the topic matters
- A realistic smaller version of the project
- A public post that introduces the idea
- Enough clarity to continue developing it during the cohort

For now, the project I can stand behind is:

**Open Source Readiness Field Guide**

A practical starting point for helping small and midsize companies understand open source usage, responsibility, compliance, sustainability, and good citizenship.

## Human Edge

The skills I am relying on that I do not believe AI can replace are judgment, trust-building, context, and community understanding.

AI can help structure information, summarize complex topics, and create drafts. But it cannot fully understand the regional context, the specific concerns of local companies, or the trust needed to bring people into a topic like open source responsibility.

Especially in topics such as compliance, licenses, SBOMs, and software supply chain, accuracy and judgment matter. It is not enough to generate content. The content has to be useful, responsible, and appropriate for the audience.

The human edge is in listening, translating, connecting, and creating trust.

## AI and uncertainty

One fear I have about AI changing my field is that it may increase the amount of content while lowering the average level of trust.

In education, DevRel, open source, and even topics like school-level math, it is now very easy to generate explanations, tutorials, checklists, and guides. But not all generated material is correct, contextual, responsible, or approachable.

For open source topics, this matters a lot. A vague or incorrect explanation of licenses, compliance, or software supply chain practices can create false confidence.

My concern is not simply that AI will replace writing or education. My concern is that increased reliance on AI will make it more difficult to identify where careful, trusted, human-curated guidance is important or essential.

## Unlearning and learning

Something I still need to unlearn is that drafting has to start from an empty page.

AI tools can help me get from a rough idea to a structure faster. They can help me test different framings, find gaps, and make complex topics more understandable. But, like every tool, they require clear intent.

I need to know who I am writing for, what problem I am solving, and what level of confidence or caution is needed.

One of the first lessons from early AI experiments is that framing is a core skill. The better I can describe the audience, context, constraints, and desired outcome, the more useful AI becomes.

That connects strongly to DevRel itself. Good DevRel is not just about producing content and marketing. It is about understanding people, context, storytelling, and the next useful step.
