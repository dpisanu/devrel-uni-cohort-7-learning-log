# Activities: Week 01 — Project Framing and Self-Reflection

## Activity overview

**Week:** 01  
**Theme:** Building and DevRel in the Age of AI  
**Activity type:** Miro collaboration, brainstorming, project framing, reflection 
**Main outcome:** Turning a broad open source / OSPO interest into a smaller project direction
**Status:** Draft  

## Miro activity

The Miro session started with a structured collection around this question:

> Which project have you wanted to do for a long time?

The cohort shared a wide range of ideas. Some were wishes, some were concrete product ideas, some were variations or add-ons to existing work and some were more meta topics such as community building.

The activity then developed into a self-reflection exercise similar in spirit to an Eisenhower matrix, but with five fields instead of four quadrants.

The fields were:

1. A project I wanted to do for a long time
2. Its mini-version that I can achieve in the next 5 weeks
3. What resources do I need?
4. What stopped me from doing it so far?
5. What will I do differently now to finish the mini-project?

### Field 1: A project I wanted to do for a long time

This field captured the larger project idea, the thing that has been sitting in the background for a while.

### Field 2: Its mini-version that I can achieve in the next 5 weeks

This field forced the project to become smaller and more realistic.

The important question was not “What is the full version of this idea?” but:

> What is the smallest meaningful version I can actually finish or demonstrate during the cohort?

### Field 3: What resources do I need?

This field focused on practical requirements such as time, tools, collaborators, knowledge, examples, templates, feedback, or access to platforms.

### Field 4: What stopped me from doing it so far?

This field surfaced blockers, such as lack of time, unclear scope, perfectionism, missing structure, fear of publishing, or lack of accountability.

### Field 5: What will I make differently now to finish the mini-project in the next 5 weeks?

This field turned the reflection into a commitment.

It asked what needs to change in behavior, process, or scope so that the project can actually move forward.

## My input to the exercise

### Field 1: A project I wanted to do for a long time

I wanted to become more engaged with the Open Source Program Office community for a long time.

This interest became stronger after attending a Linux Foundation event focused on open source in industry and completing the Open Source Software Developer certification.

The broader topic I wanted to work on was:

- ✅ Open source in industry
- ✅ OSPO practices
- ❌ Compliance and licenses
- ✅ Software Bills of Materials
- ❔ Software supply chain
- ✅ Open source sustainability
- ✅ Good open source citizenship
- ✅ Knowledge transfer to small and midsize companies (open source education for companies in my region)

### Field 2: Its mini-version

How to come from the "project I wanted to do for a while" to a mini version? This sounds more like a product.

First thought: **Open Source Readiness Field Guide**

A practical starting point for helping reflect and understand open source usage, responsibility, compliance, sustainability and good citizenship.

This smaller version is more concrete than “getting involved with the OSPO community” and easier to explain as a DevRel-style enablement artifact.

### Field 3: What resources do I need?

Useful resources for this project include:

- My notes from the Linux Foundation open source event
- Knowledge from the Open Source courses from the Linux Foundation Academy
- Public resources from the Linux Foundation and other open source organizations
- Conversations with OSPO practitioners
- Feedback from open source community members
- Examples of open source policies and readiness checklists
- Public material around SBOMs and software supply chain
- [Tough one] Insights from regional companies and their actual questions
- Lots of magic glue to hold it together

### Field 4: What stopped me from doing it so far?

Fact: The sheer size of this undertaking!

Some quick ones I could think of:
- The topic felt too broad, generic or already solved?
- Open source compliance and governance is just not my thing.
- I did not want to produce something inaccurate or oversimplified.
- I did not want to produce something based upon information I might not correctly understand / interpret.
- I was unsure who exactly the first audience should be.
- I was thinking too much about a complete solution instead of a first useful artifact.
- The OSPO space can feel like it belongs mostly to larger companies and experienced practitioners. What spot does an individual have here?
- I had not yet translated my interest into a concrete public output. More of a wish deep down in my gut. (this feels interesting and cool ... but ...)

### Field 5: What will I do differently now?

The main change is that I will try to start smaller.

Try framing puzzle pieces for such a practical field guide.

- Goal: Write in public before the idea is perfect
- Answer: Focus on a specific audience: small and midsize companies
- Test: Treat the first version as a conversation starter
- Todo: Ask for feedback from people with more OSPO and open source experience
- Separate my own notes and reflections from private cohort resources
- Make the project useful without signaling it is complete legal or compliance advice

## What I learned from the activity

The activity clearly showed that the important step was not to pick a smaller ambition, but to pick a smaller entry point.

The topic **epic** is still open source readiness and responsible participation.

The first artifact needs to be much smaller: a stepping stone towards a guide that helps companies (? or individuals ?) orient themselves and ask better questions.

That is a much more realistic and useful starting point.

So, this turns out that this project will probably accompany me throughout the the five-week cohort project and hence needs to be small enough to finish, but meaningful enough to create momentum.

But is this project for me or should it be one that supports the cohort itself?
