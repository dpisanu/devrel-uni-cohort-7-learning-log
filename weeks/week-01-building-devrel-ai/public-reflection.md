# Public Reflection: Week 01

## One-sentence summary

Week 1 forced me turn to turn a broad interest in open source, OSPO work into a concrete project framing. An Open Source Readiness Field Guide for small (and midsize) companies with the goal pf regional industry enablement.

## What I brought into the week

I came into DevRel University Cohort 7 with open source very much on my mind.

Just before the cohort started, I attended a Linux Foundation event focused on open source in industry. The event brought together regional Open Source Program Officers and Open Source Officers and the conversations focused on topics such as licenses, compliance, SBOMs, software supply chain, processes, and sustainability.

Shortly after that, I completed my Open Source Software Developer certification through the Linux Foundation.

Those two experiences made me want to engage more deeply with the OSPO community and with the question of how open source knowledge can reach companies that may not yet have formal open source structures.

## What changed during Week 1

Before the session, my idea was still broad.

I knew I was interested in:

- Open source in industry
- OSPO communities
- Regional companies
- Open source sustainability
- Responsible usage and contribution
- Knowledge sharing

The Week 1 project-framing exercise helped me turn that into a more concrete direction.

Instead of trying to work on “open source education” in general, I started framing a specific artifact:

**Open Source Readiness of understanding Field Guide**

This gives the idea more of a shape.

It also makes it easier to explain who it is for and why it matters.

## How this connects to DevRel

This topic feels deeply connected to DevRel.

Developer Relations is not only about promoting tools. It is also about education, trust, community, enablement and helping people take the next useful step.

The open source readiness topic needs exactly that kind of work.

Small (and midsize) companies may already depend on open source, but they may not yet have the language or structures to think about:

- Responsible usage
- Contribution
- License awareness
- Compliance
- Software supply chain
- Sustainability
- Community participation

A good DevRel approach can help translate these topics into something understandable and actionable.

## How this connects to AI

The Week 1 theme also asked us to think about building and DevRel in the age of AI.

For me, this raised an important tension.

AI can help produce more content, faster. But in topics like open source compliance,  the positive effect of open source, people in open source and sustainability, faster content is not automatically better content.

* Trust matters
* Accuracy matters
* Context matters
* Human judgment matters

This makes the DevRel role even more important. AI can support drafting and structure, but it cannot replace the responsibility of understanding the audience and communicating carefully.

## My takeaway

My main takeaway from Week 1 is that I do not need to start with a complete framework.

I can start by creating a clear, practical, beginner-friendly entry point.

The project I can stand behind is:

**Open Source Readiness Field Guide**  
A practical starting point for helping small and midsize companies understand open source usage, responsibility, compliance, sustainability and **good citizenship**.
