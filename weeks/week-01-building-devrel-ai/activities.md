# Activities: Week 01 — Project Framing and Self-Reflection

## Activity overview

**Week:** 01  
**Theme:** Building and DevRel in the Age of AI  
**Activity type:** Miro collaboration, brainstorming, project framing, reflection  
**Main outcome:** Turning a broad open source / OSPO interest into a smaller project direction  
**Status:** Draft

## Miro activity

The Miro activity started with a structured reflection around this question:

> Which project have you wanted to do for a long time?

The exercise then moved through five fields:

1. A project I wanted to do for a long time
2. Its mini-version that I can achieve during the cohort
3. What resources do I need?
4. What stopped me from doing it so far?
5. What will I do differently now to finish the mini-project?

This file captures my working input from that activity. The more polished project framing and retrospective lives in [`project-brief.md`](./project-brief.md).

## My input to the exercise

### Field 1: A project I wanted to do for a long time

I wanted to become more engaged with the Open Source Program Office community.

This interest became stronger after attending a Linux Foundation event focused on open source in industry and completing the Open Source Software Developer certification.

The broader topic I wanted to work on was:

- ✅ Open source in industry
- ✅ OSPO practices
- ✅ Software Bills of Materials
- ❔ Software supply chain
- ✅ Open source sustainability
- ✅ Good open source citizenship
- ✅ Knowledge transfer to small and midsize companies
- ✅ Open source education for companies in my region

I am interested in compliance and licenses because they matter, but they are also the part of the topic where I need the most care, context, and expert feedback.

### Field 2: Its mini-version

The mini-version became:

**Open Source Readiness Field Guide**

A practical starting point for helping small and midsize companies reflect on and understand open source usage, responsibility, compliance, sustainability, software supply chain topics, and good citizenship.

This smaller version is more concrete than “getting involved with the OSPO community” and easier to explain as a DevRel-style enablement artifact.

### Field 3: What resources do I need?

Useful resources for this project include:

- My notes from the Linux Foundation open source event
- Knowledge from Open Source courses from the Linux Foundation Academy
- Public resources from the Linux Foundation and other open source organizations
- Conversations with OSPO practitioners
- Feedback from open source community members
- Examples of open source policies and readiness checklists
- Public material around SBOMs and software supply chain
- Insights from regional companies and their actual questions
- Time to turn a large topic into practical, understandable pieces

### Field 4: What stopped me from doing it so far?

The main blocker was the size of the topic.

Some specific blockers:

- The topic felt too broad, generic, or already solved.
- Open source compliance and governance felt intimidating.
- I did not want to produce something inaccurate or oversimplified.
- I was unsure who the first audience should be.
- I was thinking too much about a complete solution instead of a first useful artifact.
- The OSPO space can feel like it belongs mostly to larger companies and experienced practitioners.
- I had not yet translated my interest into a concrete public output.

### Field 5: What will I do differently now?

The main change is that I will start smaller.

Instead of trying to cover the entire topic, I will frame puzzle pieces for a practical field guide:

- Write in public before the idea is perfect.
- Focus on a specific audience: small and midsize companies.
- Treat the first version as a conversation starter.
- Ask for feedback from people with more OSPO and open source experience.
- Separate my own notes and reflections from private cohort resources.
- Make the project useful without presenting it as complete legal or compliance advice.

## What I learned from the activity

The activity showed that the important step was not to pick a smaller ambition. It was to pick a smaller entry point.

The broader topic is still open source readiness and responsible participation. The first artifact needs to be much smaller: a stepping stone toward a guide that helps companies orient themselves and ask better questions.

This project may or may not continue throughout all five weeks. For now, I will treat the project brief as a weekly snapshot so changes in direction are visible instead of hidden.
