# Activities: Week 02 — Career Direction and Focus

## Activity context

The Week 02 activity again centered around a Miro board with five reflection areas. 

## Miro prompts

The activity asked participants to reflect on:

1. What you think is changing in tech, from your own perspective rather than a generic answer.
2. What you are choosing to focus on, including skills, areas, and problems.
3. What you are deliberately not focusing on.
4. What you want to become known for in the next six months.
5. How AI fits into your workflow.

## What changed or clarified my thinking

The activity showed that my project is not currently blocked by a lack of ideas. It is blocked by too many possible directions.

The strongest project-related realization was that the **Open Source Readiness Field Guide** should not start as a blank-page writing project. It should start with a map of existing material.

This matters because there are already many open source resources, trainings, curricula and OSPO-related guides. The value of my project may come from:

- finding them
- comparing them
- sequencing them
- identifying gaps
- adapting them to a local SME context
- creating an approachable path through them

## Activity tension

The activity also surfaced a tension between depth and accessibility.

I want to avoid spending too much time on low-level explanations like “What is Git?” or “What is a repository?” But those basics connect to a more important point: collaboration around a shared source of truth, transparent change history and strategies for combining work.

I also want to avoid becoming too legalistic around licensing, intellectual property and ownership. But those areas are central to open source. They are part of what makes open source powerful and also part of where organizations can make serious mistakes.

So the project needs to explain why these topics matter without pretending to replace legal, compliance, or OSPO expertise.

## How AI fits into the work

AI helped most with fast content consumption and summarization.

A rough workflow emerged:

1. Collect links and source material.
2. Ask AI for first-pass summaries.
3. Try to normalize different sources into comparable datasets.
4. Compare coverage and depth.
5. Identify which resources may matter for the field guide.

The first two steps were easy. The later steps were harder.

The more links I added, the more the AI output risked becoming cluttered. The difficult part was not summarization. The difficult part was giving the AI enough direction to follow a clear line of inquiry.

## Unfinished areas and open questions

- What dimensions should the source inventory compare?
- Which resources are mature enough to become primary references?
- Which resources are too generic for the field guide?
- How much licensing and IP context belongs in the first version?
- How can I explain open source collaboration without turning the guide into a source versioning or tool tutorial?
- How can I keep the project local and practical while still relying on existing global open source resources?

## Activity outcome

The activity moved the project from “build a guide” toward “first build a map.”

That is smaller but a more honest result. The first tangible version is not a polished guide, but a rough research map and source inventory built from a long link collection, physical post-it notes and AI-assisted source summaries.
